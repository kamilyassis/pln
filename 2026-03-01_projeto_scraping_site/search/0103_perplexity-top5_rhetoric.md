# What are the Top 5 General levers for persuasive speeches?

The five most general levers in persuasive speeches are: ethos (credibility), pathos (emotion), logos (logic), social proof/identity, and rhetorical patterning (repetition, contrast, storytelling). Each can be used either ethically or manipulatively, depending on intent. [en.wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modes_of_persuasion)

## Ethos (credibility)

Ethos is the speaker’s perceived character, competence, and trustworthiness; audiences are more easily led by someone they see as knowledgeable and morally sound. In practice, this is built through credentials, confident delivery, shared values, and demonstrating fairness, which lowers critical resistance to the message. [virtualspeech](https://virtualspeech.com/blog/ethos-pathos-logos-public-speaking-persuasion)

## Pathos (emotion)

Pathos is the deliberate triggering of emotions (fear, hope, anger, pride, belonging) to move the audience toward a desired attitude or action. Speakers use vivid language, stories, and emotionally loaded framing so that listeners “feel” before they scrutinize, which makes them more susceptible to influence. [skillsyouneed](https://www.skillsyouneed.com/ips/rhetorical-techniques.html)

## Logos (logic and evidence)

Logos is the appeal to reason: coherent arguments, clear cause–effect chains, data, and examples that make the conclusion seem inevitable. Well-structured points that progress logically, backed by evidence the audience accepts, create an impression that “the facts” support the speaker’s preferred outcome. [amanet](https://www.amanet.org/articles/three-elements-of-persuasion/)

## Social proof and identity

Social proof uses cues about what “others like you” think or do (majorities, experts, in‑group members) to push people to align with the group. When the audience feels the speaker and message are part of “our side” (liking, unity, shared identity), defenses drop and conformity pressure does the rest. [cognitigence](https://www.cognitigence.com/blog/cialdini-7-principles-of-persuasion)

## Rhetorical patterning

Rhetorical patterning covers surface techniques—repetition (anaphora), parallelism, vivid contrast, and tight slogans—that make ideas stick and feel more true. Devices like “I have a dream…”, “of the people, by the people, for the people”, and sharp juxtapositions create rhythm and memorability, which amplifies the persuasive impact of the underlying message. [ucd](https://www.ucd.ie/writingcentre/t4media/Persuasive%20Techniques%20in%20Speeches%20SoRh.pdf)
